Prior Projects

Biogeochemistry of Toxic Metals

I and my student group have been investigating arsenic chemistry in hot springs and streams in the Atacama desert region of Chile for more than 10 years, and the biologic and abiotic reactions that control As transport, transformation, and loss to the sediments. This project involved field analyses in extreme environments and laboratory microcosm experiments to document the redox chemistry of arsenic.

In an international investigation my team identified a set of lakes in Mongolia with the highest reported natural concentration of Uranium. We investigated the source and transport of dissolved U, and the fate once it discharged to the lakes. This project eventually involved extensive communications with the Mongolian Environment Ministry to convey to them the potential health hazards associated with U-rich dust.

I worked with the Petitioners in a SOAH hearing and defendants in an associated lawsuit to establish a new in-situ uranium mine in Texas.

Petroleum Transport and Biodegradation

I worked on the US Geological Survey Bemidji research site for more than 25 years investigating the degradation of petroleum my bacteria, and the reactions between the bacteria and the rocks as a method of tracing the contaminant plume and rate of natural attenuation.

In a lawsuit against a pipeline company I worked with the plaintiffs to show when a pipeline started leaking and how the oil moved into the Ogallala aquifer and began to degrade. I used fluid and geochemical models and water chemistry analysis.

Salt Water Contamination

With an international team investigated the dynamics of salt water infiltration and aquifer contamination associated with the storm surge from Typhoon Haiyan in the Philippines

Acted as an expert witness on several lawsuits involving salt water contamination of shallow water wells due to oil and gas field operations.

Chlorinated Solvent Contamination

In a class-action lawsuit against 25 separate defendants in an industrial park in Illinois, I worked with the Plaintiffs to identify the source, transport, and degradation of chlorinated solvents in an aquifer that supplied water to over 1000 families.

Karst Hydrology and Microbiology

I worked with an Austin-based environmental group to show the hazards of placing a new development with plans to use land application of treated waste water over the karstic recharge area of the Edwards Aquifer.

I have been working intensively for more than 10 years on cave and karst development involving microbial processes.

Soil Gas

I have had more than a dozen different projects involving soil gas source, movement, and fate, and using soil gas to map dissolved contaminant plumes. Some examples:

  • Tracing the source of CO2 in soils
  • Identifying soil microbial processes by mapping atmospheric gases
  • Measuring the rate of hydrogen sulfide oxidation by bacteria in a cave
  • Using molecular hydrogen to identify microbial redox zones
  • Using gas as a soil tracer
  • Identified microbial processes in arctic permafrost associated with methane production and consumption

Co-developer of method of using gas concentration ratios to identify leaking CO2 sequestration reservoirs (patent pending)

Rock-Water-Gas Geochemistry

With my team I recently investigated the controls on CO2 solubility in mixtures of water and inorganic salts. Developed a new model method for accurately estimating CO2 solubility in saline reservoirs associated with geologic sequestration.

Identified the source and fate of dissolved manganese contaminating hundreds of water wells in the Nile Delta region of Egypt

For almost 35 years have worked on the ability of microbes to attack and decompose minerals and rocks in a variety of settings.

I have used geochemical modeling in a wide variety of geochemical scenarios to trace contaminants and identify chemical reactions including sorption and ion exchange, gas dissolution, rock-water reactions and reaction rate, and transport of dissolved solutes in groundwater.

Subsurface Microbiology

I have worked for more than 25 years researching microbial processes in aquifers and sediments, and how microorganisms mobilize metals, dissolve rocks, and change aquifer properties. I use classical microscopy and culturing methods as well as advanced electron microscopy and high throughput molecular methods to identify microbial communities and what microbes are doing to the rocks.
